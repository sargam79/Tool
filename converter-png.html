Image Format Guide: PNG vs JPG vs WebP

Choosing the right image format is essential for web performance, storage efficiency, and visual quality. Different formats excel in different scenarios. Understanding when to use PNG, JPG, WebP, or PDF helps you optimize images for web, print, and archival purposes.

What are image formats? Image formats are ways of encoding visual data. Each format compresses images differently, affecting quality, file size, and compatibility. PNG preserves perfect quality but creates larger files. JPG compresses aggressively for smaller file sizes. WebP offers the best balance of quality and compression. PDF is ideal for documents and printing.

Why convert images? You might need to:

  • Optimize for web: Convert PNG/BMP to JPG/WebP for faster loading
  • Reduce file size: Compress for email, storage, or sharing
  • Change format: Convert for compatibility with specific software
  • Prepare for print: Convert to PDF with proper quality and color space
  • Archive efficiently: Compress images while maintaining acceptable quality

Format comparison: PNG is ideal for graphics and images needing transparency. JPG is best for photographs and complex images. WebP offers superior compression for modern browsers. PDF is perfect for documents and printing. Each format has specific use cases.

💡 Expert Image Conversion Tips

  • Use JPG for photographs (smaller files, acceptable quality loss)
  • Use PNG for graphics, logos, or images needing transparency
  • Use WebP for modern web (best compression, supported by all browsers now)
  • Quality 85% is sweet spot for JPG/WebP—good quality without bloated files
  • PNG is lossless—convert to JPG to reduce size, but never convert JPG→PNG (won't improve quality)
  • For web, optimize images: use JPG 85% for photos, WebP for complex graphics
  • Always keep an original copy—converting JPG to PNG won't restore lost data
  • Test quality settings—convert, download, and compare file sizes vs visual quality

Frequently Asked Questions

Will converting to JPG reduce quality? ▼

JPG uses lossy compression, so yes—some quality is lost. But at 85-90% quality, the loss is imperceptible to most people while file size drops 50-80%. Test different quality levels to find your sweet spot.

Can I convert JPG back to PNG and restore quality? ▼

No. JPG compresses data destructively—once lost, it's gone forever. Converting JPG→PNG just changes the file format but won't restore lost quality. Always keep the original file before converting.

What's the maximum file size? ▼

This converter handles files up to 50MB. For larger files, use desktop image editor software like Photoshop or free alternatives like GIMP.

Does PNG transparency convert to other formats? ▼

WebP and PDF preserve transparency. JPG doesn't support transparency—transparent areas become white background. If you need transparency, convert to PNG or WebP instead.

What's the difference between JPG quality settings? ▼

Quality scale (1-100%) controls compression: 95%+ = highest quality, huge file; 85% = recommended balance; 70% = good for web, noticeably smaller; 50% = visible quality loss, very small file. Test different levels for your images.

Is WebP supported by all browsers? ▼

Yes, WebP is now supported by all modern browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge). It offers better compression than JPG while maintaining quality. Ideal for websites.

Is my image data private? ▼

Yes, 100% private. All conversions happen in your browser. No files uploaded to servers, no data collection, no tracking. Your images stay completely private.
