Understanding Your Paycheck: Gross to Net Income

Your paycheck can be confusing. You earn a certain salary, but after taxes and deductions, the amount you actually take home is significantly less. Understanding how taxes work and what comes out of your paycheck is essential for budgeting and financial planning.

What's the difference between gross and net pay? Your gross salary is your total earnings before any deductions. Your net pay (take-home) is what you actually receive after federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and other deductions are subtracted. The difference can be substantial—someone earning $60,000 might only take home $45,000.

What taxes affect your paycheck? Multiple taxes take a portion of your income:

  • Federal Income Tax: Progressive tax system (6-37% depending on income bracket)
  • Social Security: 6.2% of gross income (capped at $168,600 in 2023)
  • Medicare: 1.45% of all income plus 0.9% additional on high earners
  • State Income Tax: Varies by state (0-13.3% depending on location)
  • Other Deductions: 401(k), health insurance, FSA, HSA, etc.

Why use a salary calculator? Knowing your take-home pay helps you budget accurately, understand tax liability, plan for expenses, and make informed career decisions. When comparing job offers, net pay matters more than gross salary.

💰 Expert Financial Tips

  • Use take-home pay (net) for budgeting, not gross salary—that's what you actually spend
  • Maximize 401(k) contributions—they reduce taxable income, lowering your tax bill immediately
  • Adjust W-4 withholding if you're getting large refunds—you're giving the government an interest-free loan
  • Consider HSA contributions for tax-free healthcare savings—triple tax advantage (deductible, grows tax-free, withdrawals for medical are tax-free)
  • When comparing job offers, compare net pay, not gross—location and benefits matter for your actual take-home
  • Track actual paychecks for 3 months—verify this calculator matches your real net pay
  • Plan for taxes quarterly if self-employed or have side income—use this calculator for estimates
  • Review tax withholding annually—major life changes (marriage, kids, new job) affect your taxes significantly

Frequently Asked Questions

How is federal income tax calculated? ▼

Federal income tax is progressive using tax brackets. Each bracket has a percentage rate. You pay 10% on the first $11,000 (if single), 12% on the next bracket, etc. Your "average tax rate" is total taxes divided by income (usually 12-24% for most workers). The calculator uses accurate 2024 brackets.

What are Social Security and Medicare taxes? ▼

FICA taxes (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) fund Social Security and Medicare. You pay 6.2% for Social Security (capped at $168,600 income in 2024) and 1.45% Medicare on all income. Your employer matches these, but you only see your portion deducted from paychecks. Total is 15.3% when including employer's share.

Why is my take-home pay less than expected? ▼

Multiple taxes stack up: federal (10-24%), state (0-13%), Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), plus any pre-tax deductions. Combined, taxes often take 25-35% of gross income. Someone earning $60,000 might only take home $42,000-45,000. This calculator shows the breakdown so you understand where each dollar goes.

Should I increase my withholding or get a larger refund? ▼

If you regularly get large refunds (over $2,000), you're over-withholding—the government is holding your money interest-free. Adjust your W-4 to have less withheld, giving you more in each paycheck. If you owe taxes, you're under-withholding. Aim to owe or get refunded less than $500.

How accurate is this calculator? ▼

Very accurate for federal and FICA taxes using 2024 brackets. State taxes are simplified—some states have local taxes not included. If this shows $45,000 net but you take home $43,500, the difference is likely pre-tax deductions (401k, insurance) not entered. Compare with actual paychecks to verify accuracy for your situation.

What deductions can I claim to reduce taxes? ▼

Pre-tax deductions: 401(k) contributions, health insurance premiums, FSA, HSA, child support, union dues. These reduce taxable income before taxes are calculated. Post-tax deductions (Roth 401k, charitable donations) don't reduce income tax but may reduce AGI. The calculator includes an "Additional Deductions" field for these pre-tax amounts.
