How It Works

We use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation (the most accurate BMR formula validated by research) to calculate your Basal Metabolic Rate. Then we multiply by your activity level to get TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure). Finally, we adjust for your goal: -500 calories for weight loss, +500 for weight gain, or maintenance calories. Macro ratios are calculated based on your target calories and selected diet preference.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about calorie calculation

How do I calculate my daily calorie needs? ▼
Daily calorie needs are calculated using your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) multiplied by an activity factor. BMR is the calories your body burns at rest. We use the Mifflin-St Jeor formula for accuracy. Then multiply by your activity level: Sedentary (1.2), Lightly Active (1.375), Moderately Active (1.55), Very Active (1.725), or Extra Active (1.9).
What is the difference between BMR and TDEE? ▼
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest to maintain basic life functions. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is your total calorie burn including all activities, exercise, and digestion. TDEE = BMR x Activity Factor.
How many calories should I eat to lose weight? ▼
To lose weight, create a calorie deficit of 500-1000 calories below your TDEE. This typically results in 1-2 pounds (0.5-1kg) of weight loss per week. A 500-calorie deficit is recommended for sustainable, healthy weight loss without muscle loss.
What are macros and why do they matter? ▼
Macros (macronutrients) are Protein, Carbohydrates, and Fats. They matter because: Protein builds and repairs muscle (0.7-1g per lb body weight), Carbs provide energy for workouts and brain function, and Fats support hormones and cell health. The ideal ratio depends on your goals: Weight Loss (40% Protein, 30% Carbs, 30% Fat), Maintenance (30% Protein, 40% Carbs, 30% Fat), Muscle Gain (35% Protein, 45% Carbs, 20% Fat).
Is this calorie calculator accurate? ▼
Yes, our calculator uses the scientifically validated Mifflin-St Jeor equation (1990) which is considered the most accurate BMR formula for the general population. It has been validated in numerous studies and is used by dietitians and nutritionists worldwide. Results are typically accurate within +/- 10%.
