How to Calculate Your Daily Calorie Needs [Complete Guide 2026]

How many calories do you actually need each day? It's one of the most common nutrition questions — and one of the most misunderstood. Eat too few and you lose muscle and slow your metabolism. What Is TDEE and Why It Matters?

Your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is the total number of calories your body burns in a day — including everything from breathing and digestion to exercise and daily movement. It is the most important number in nutrition because it defines your calorie "maintenance" level.

  • Eat at your TDEE → Your weight stays the same
  • Eat below your TDEE → You lose weight (fat loss)
  • Eat above your TDEE → You gain weight (muscle or fat)

TDEE is made up of four components: your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR), physical activity, the thermic effect of food (digestion burns ~10% of calories consumed), and non-exercise activity like fidgeting and walking around.

Step 1: Calculate Your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest — just to keep your heart beating, lungs breathing, and organs functioning. It accounts for roughly 60–70% of your total calorie burn.

The most accurate widely-used formula is the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics:

📐 Mifflin-St Jeor Formula
For Men:
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5

For Women:
BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

BMR Example (Man, 30 years, 80 kg, 175 cm)

BMR = (10 × 80) + (6.25 × 175) − (5 × 30) + 5
= 800 + 1,093.75 − 150 + 5 = 1,748 calories/day

BMR Example (Woman, 28 years, 65 kg, 165 cm)

BMR = (10 × 65) + (6.25 × 165) − (5 × 28) − 161
= 650 + 1,031.25 − 140 − 161 = 1,380 calories/day

💡 BMR Is Not Your Daily Calorie Target

BMR only accounts for resting calories. You need to multiply it by your activity level to find your true daily needs (TDEE). Eating only your BMR calories would be dangerously low for most active people.

Step 2: Multiply by Your Activity Level

Multiply your BMR by the activity multiplier that best matches your lifestyle to get your TDEE (maintenance calories):

Activity LevelDescriptionMultiplier
SedentaryDesk job, little or no exercise× 1.2
Lightly ActiveLight exercise 1–3 days/week× 1.375
Moderately ActiveModerate exercise 3–5 days/week× 1.55
Very ActiveHard exercise 6–7 days/week× 1.725
Extra ActiveVery hard exercise + physical job× 1.9

Using our example man (BMR = 1,748) who exercises moderately 4 days/week:
TDEE = 1,748 × 1.55 = 2,709 calories/day

This is how many calories he needs to eat to maintain his current weight.

Step 3: Adjust for Your Goal

Once you know your TDEE, adjusting for your goal is simple:

🔥

Fat Loss

TDEE − 300 to 500

Creates a safe deficit for steady fat loss of 0.3–0.5 kg/week without sacrificing muscle

⚖️

Maintenance

= TDEE

Eat at your TDEE to maintain your current weight and body composition

💪

Muscle Gain

TDEE + 200 to 350

A modest surplus supports lean muscle growth without excessive fat gain

⚠️ Avoid Extreme Deficits

Cutting more than 500–750 calories below TDEE often causes muscle loss, extreme hunger, fatigue, and a slowdown in metabolism. Slower, consistent deficits win long-term. Never go below 1,200 calories (women) or 1,500 calories (men) without medical supervision.

Calorie Quality vs. Calorie Quantity

Hitting your calorie target is important — but what makes up those calories matters too. 2,000 calories of whole foods fuels your body very differently than 2,000 calories of junk food, even if the numbers match.

Prioritize These Foods

  • Protein: Chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, legumes — preserves muscle, keeps you full
  • Complex carbohydrates: Oats, rice, sweet potato, fruit — sustained energy
  • Healthy fats: Avocado, nuts, olive oil, salmon — hormones and brain health
  • Vegetables: High volume, low calorie — essential micronutrients and fiber

Limit These Foods

  • Ultra-processed snacks and fast food (calorie-dense, nutrient-poor)
  • Sugary drinks — liquid calories don't trigger fullness signals
  • Refined carbohydrates that cause blood sugar spikes and crashes

Pro Tips for Hitting Your Calorie Target

1

Track Your Food (Even Briefly)

Use a food tracking app like MyFitnessPal for 2–4 weeks. Most people are shocked at how many calories they consume versus what they think. Tracking builds accurate awareness without needing to do it forever.

2

Eat High-Volume, Low-Calorie Foods

Vegetables, fruits, lean proteins, and soups have high water and fiber content — they fill your stomach without filling your calorie budget. You'll feel full while staying in your target range.

3

Don't Drink Your Calories

Juice, soda, lattes, smoothies, and alcohol can easily add 300–600 hidden calories per day. Switching to water, black coffee, or plain tea is one of the fastest ways to reduce calorie intake.

4

Meal Prep to Stay Consistent

Preparing meals in advance removes the decision fatigue that leads to poor food choices. If healthy food is ready, you're far more likely to hit your target — especially on busy days.

5

Recalculate Every 4–6 Weeks

As your weight changes, so does your TDEE. Recalculate your calorie needs every month or whenever your weight changes by more than 3–4 kg to keep your targets accurate.

💡 Stay Hydrated

Thirst is often mistaken for hunger. Drinking 2–3 litres of water per day reduces unnecessary snacking and keeps metabolism running efficiently. Use our free water intake calculator to find your daily hydration target.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories should I eat per day to lose weight?
For safe, steady fat loss, eat 300–500 calories below your TDEE (maintenance calories). This creates a weekly deficit of 2,100–3,500 calories, resulting in roughly 0.3–0.5 kg of fat loss per week. Use our free calorie calculator to find your exact target.
What is the difference between BMR and TDEE?
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the calories you burn at complete rest — just to stay alive. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is your total daily burn including all activity. TDEE = BMR × activity multiplier. Your daily calorie target should be based on TDEE, not BMR.
How accurate are online calorie calculators?
Research shows the Mifflin-St Jeor equation is accurate within 10% for most people. That's a solid starting estimate. However, individual metabolism varies — use the calculator as your starting point, then adjust based on real-world results over 2–3 weeks.
Do I need to count calories forever to stay healthy?
Not necessarily. Tracking calories for a few weeks helps you build accurate food awareness. Many people then successfully maintain their goals by eating mindfully, following consistent meal patterns, and checking in with a calculator periodically rather than tracking every day.
Why am I not losing weight even in a calorie deficit?
Common reasons include underestimating food portions, overestimating activity, water retention (especially early on), or metabolic adaptation over time. Try weighing food with a kitchen scale for accuracy, recalculate your TDEE if your weight has changed significantly, and ensure you're getting adequate sleep.
How many calories do I need to build muscle?
For lean muscle gain with minimal fat, eat 200–350 calories above your TDEE. Combined with consistent strength training and high protein intake (1.6–2.2g/kg of bodyweight), this modest surplus supports muscle growth without excessive fat gain.

Conclusion

Knowing your daily calorie needs is the foundation of any successful nutrition plan — whether your goal is fat loss, weight maintenance, or building muscle. The process is simple: calculate your BMR, multiply by your activity level to get your TDEE, then adjust up or down based on your goal.
