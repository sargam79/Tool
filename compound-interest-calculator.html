Understanding Compound Interest

Compound interest is often called the eighth wonder of the world — and for good reason. It is the process by which your investment earnings generate their own earnings, creating exponential growth over time. Unlike simple interest, which pays only on the principal, compound interest pays on both the principal and accumulated interest.

The Compound Interest Formula

The standard compound interest formula is:

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Where:

A = Final amount

P = Principal (initial investment)

r = Annual interest rate (decimal)

n = Number of times interest compounds per year

t = Number of years

For most investment scenarios with monthly contributions, the calculation becomes more complex. This calculator uses the future value of a series formula to account for regular deposits:

Future Value = P(1 + r)^t + PMT × [((1 + r)^t − 1) / r]

Where PMT = monthly contribution and r = monthly rate (annual rate ÷ 12)

The Rule of 72

The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math trick to estimate how long an investment takes to double:

Years to double = 72 ÷ Annual return rate (%)

Examples:

  • At 6% return: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double
  • At 8% return: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double
  • At 10% return: 72 ÷ 10 = 7.2 years to double
  • At 12% return: 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years to double

The Rule of 72 is remarkably accurate for returns between 6% and 10%. It becomes less precise at extremes but remains useful for quick estimates.

The Power of Starting Early

Consider two investors who both contribute $500 monthly at 8% annual return:

  • Investor A: Starts at age 25, contributes until 35 (10 years), then stops. Total contributed: $60,000. At age 65: $1,006,000
  • Investor B: Starts at age 35, contributes until 65 (30 years). Total contributed: $180,000. At age 65: $745,000

Investor A contributed one-third as much money but ends with 35% more wealth because their money had 10 extra years to compound. This is why financial advisors emphasize starting to invest as early as possible, even with small amounts.

Common Mistakes That Reduce Compound Growth

  • Withdrawing early: Taking money out interrupts compounding and may trigger taxes and penalties.
  • Chasing high returns: Promises of 20%+ returns usually indicate scams or extremely risky investments. Sustainable wealth building requires patience.
  • Not reinvesting dividends: Dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs) automatically compound returns. Cash dividends spent elsewhere slow growth.
  • High fees: A 1% annual fee on a $100,000 portfolio costs over $100,000 in lost growth over 30 years. Use low-cost index funds when possible.
  • Timing the market: Missing just the 10 best market days over 20 years can reduce returns by 50%. Consistent investing beats perfect timing.
