What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple screening tool that uses your height and weight to estimate whether you are underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese. It was developed in the 1830s by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet and has been used by health organizations worldwide for over a century.

The formula is straightforward: BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². For imperial units, the formula is: BMI = (weight (lb) / height (in)²) × 703.

Why BMI Categories Differ for Some Populations

The WHO standards above apply to adults of European descent. Research has shown that BMI thresholds should be adjusted for different ethnic groups:

  • Asian populations: Health risks begin at lower BMI levels. The WHO Western Pacific Region uses a cutoff of 23 for overweight and 25 for obesity.
  • South Asian populations: Studies show increased diabetes and cardiovascular risk at BMI levels below 25. Some health authorities use 23 as the overweight threshold.
  • Pacific Islander populations: May have higher muscle mass and bone density, making standard BMI potentially misleading.

When BMI is Misleading

BMI is a population-level screening tool, not a diagnostic measure. It can be inaccurate for individuals in these situations:

  • Athletes and bodybuilders: Muscle weighs more than fat. A muscular person may have a BMI in the "overweight" or "obese" category despite low body fat.
  • Elderly adults: Muscle mass naturally decreases with age. An older adult with normal BMI may still have excess body fat and low muscle mass (sarcopenic obesity).
  • Pregnant women: BMI is not used during pregnancy. Weight gain is expected and necessary for fetal development.
  • Children and teenagers: BMI for children is interpreted using age and sex-specific percentiles, not adult categories.
  • People with edema: Fluid retention increases weight without increasing body fat.
  • Very tall or very short individuals: BMI does not account for body frame size or proportional differences at height extremes.

Better Alternatives to BMI

For a more accurate assessment of body composition, consider these measurements alongside BMI:

  • Waist circumference: Excess abdominal fat (apple-shaped body) carries higher health risk than fat distributed in hips and thighs. Men should aim for under 40 inches (102 cm); women under 35 inches (88 cm).
  • Waist-to-hip ratio: Divide waist measurement by hip measurement. Ratios above 0.90 for men and 0.85 for women indicate increased health risk.
  • Body fat percentage: Measured by calipers, bioelectrical impedance, DEXA scan, or underwater weighing. Healthy ranges are approximately 10–20% for men and 20–30% for women.
  • Body fat distribution: Visceral fat (around organs) is more dangerous than subcutaneous fat (under skin). CT and MRI can measure this, though they are expensive.

Health Risks Associated with BMI Categories

Research consistently links higher BMI to increased risk of:

  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Cardiovascular disease and hypertension
  • Stroke
  • Certain cancers (breast, colon, kidney, liver)
  • Sleep apnea
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Fatty liver disease (NAFLD)
  • Reduced fertility

Conversely, being underweight increases risk of osteoporosis, anemia, weakened immune function, and fertility problems.

How to Use Your BMI Result

If your BMI falls outside the normal range, do not panic. A single measurement is not a diagnosis. Consider these steps:

  • Consult a healthcare provider: A doctor can assess your overall health using blood tests, physical examination, and family history.
  • Measure waist circumference: This provides additional context about where your body stores fat.
  • Track trends, not single numbers: BMI changes over time matter more than one reading. Gradual weight gain or loss indicates lifestyle patterns.
  • Focus on health behaviors: Regular physical activity, balanced nutrition, adequate sleep, and stress management improve health regardless of BMI.
